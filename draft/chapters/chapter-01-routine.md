# chapter-01-routine

One in a million, they said. That’s how I got the job. I was special.

Little did I know that out of a world with 4,096 civilizational repositories and an exact allocation of one million citizens in each, there are 4,096 one-in-a-million evaluators exactly like me. Well, 8,132 including the senior staff at Syndicate Central. Plus me. 8,133.

How I even ended up here is a rounding error. It’s not like I was at the top of my class. I wasn’t at the bottom either. I was perfectly, safely median. Just lucky, I guess. No one else from my tier started their career at Central, but then again, none of them are glorified secretaries.

"Hey Declan, how are your kids?" I asked, dropping a stack of hardware requisition slips onto his terminal desk.

"Optimal," he responded, not looking up from a heatmap of Branch 1024’s agricultural yields. "Just another day in paradise. And yours?" He didn't look up, but the edge of his mouth twitched. It was an old joke.

"I’ll tell you when I have any. Not having a repository sucks. I’m dying of boredom collating your expense reports."

"Hey, you’ll get a deployment someday," Declan said, finally switching off the heatmap. "And you’ll actually know what to do with it, having started your career here at Central. You learn the architecture first."

"How many repositories do you handle anyway?"

"Three hundred and forty-one. Wait, no, three hundred and forty-two as of the midnight synchronization. But I’m mostly high-level overview, so it’s manageable. You learn what to pay attention to. The noise filters itself out."

"Crazy," I muttered, gathering the stamped slips. "I’m working my way up to one. Just you wait. I’ll get a branch assignment before you age out and get archived."

"I’ll believe it when the ledger commits it. Anyway, take that structural integrity report over to Amina. She’s waiting on it for the noon briefing."

"Sure thing."

I navigated the white, sound-dampened corridors of the Central core. Every floor looked exactly like the one above it—perfectly optimized, low-friction, sterile.

Amina was in the Infostructure bullpen, rubbing her temples in front of a cascading wall of merge conflicts. She took the report from my hand without word, her thumb already scanning the printout for signature anomalies.

"Is the Branch 128 patch dropping today?" I asked.

"If they ever fix their local variable naming conventions," she muttered, typing a command with one hand. "Thanks. Tell Paulo he's late on his pipeline verification."

I found Paulo three sub-levels down in the physical server vaults, checking the fiber seals. The cold air down there smelled like ionized copper and synthetic coolant.

"Amina says you're late on the verification," I shouted over the hum of the cooling stacks.

Paulo didn't look down from the ladder. "Tell her the staging environment for Branch 512 is hanging on a cultural dependency loop. I'm not pushing it to prod until it clears." He tossed a dead diagnostic drive down to me. "Drop that in recycling on your way up, will you?"

Back on the administrative levels, the air was warmer and smelled faintly of the yeast-synthesized nutrient paste everyone pretended to enjoy. Nadia was at the logistics terminal, cross-referencing shipping manifests for the external transect lines.

"Recycling drop," I said, putting the drive in her bin.

"Perfect timing," Nadia said, handing me a small, sealed vial of amber liquid. "Take this to Mama Chipo in the residential quad. It’s her weekly luxury allotment. If she doesn't get her mate, she starts logging false latency reports to get attention."

The residential quad was the only place in Central that felt like it had dirt in it. Mama Chipo sat on a low bench beneath a hydroponic lemon tree, her fingers moving over a mechanical rosary that counted down system cycles instead of prayers.

"The good stuff," I said, handing over the vial.

She cracked it open, inhaling the sharp, herbal scent of the Argentine extract. "The data tells me you're late today," she grumbled, though she smiled, her wrinkled face shifting like a shifting tectonic plate. "You spend too much time talking to the machines, little ghost. Go find Leif. He's trying to calibrate the air scrubbers again and he’s going to suffocate us all."

Leif was in the maintenance plenum, half-buried in a ventilation duct, muttering curses at a pneumatic valve.

"Mama Chipo says you're going to suffocate us," I called out to his boots.

A muffled clank echoed from the duct. "Mama Chipo thinks any air that doesn't smell like charcoal is poison," Leif grunted, sliding out on his back, his face streaked with gray grease. "Hand me that torque driver. Not that one, the 4-millimeter. Thanks. Go tell Aiko she needs to re-approve the airflow parameters. The system keeps rolling back my changes."

Aiko was in the compliance office, drinking tea from a porcelain cup that looked far too fragile for a software society. She listened to Leif’s complaint without blinking.

"The system rolls it back because his parameters violate the noise-pollution threshold for the lower dorms," Aiko said smoothly, her voice a calm, unchanging baseline. "I’ll adjust the threshold by zero-point-two percent. Pass the note to Mateo down in legal. He needs to log the variance."

Mateo’s office was a graveyard of old plastic-bound policy manuals—relics from the pre-Syndicate era that he insisted on keeping for 'historical context.' He didn't look up from his ancient flat-panel display when I walked in.

"Variance request from Aiko," I said, sliding the note onto his desk.

"Another one?" Mateo sighed, his stylus scratching against a digital notepad. "We're patching the lifestyle code so much it’s starting to look like spaghetti. If Priya asks, tell her the legal framework for the Branch 2048 expansion is still blocked. The local population is refusing to sign the repository covenant."

Priya was in the strategy lounge, staring out the massive reinforced window at the grey, endless expanse of the unmapped world outside Central. The horizon was nothing but jagged granite and low, processing clouds.

"Blocked?" Priya said when I delivered the message. She didn't turn away from the window. "They'll sign. They always sign once their local infrastructure hits the depreciation cliff. Tell Wei to prepare the deployment hardware for their skeleton crew anyway. We aren't delaying the launch."

Wei was in the staging hangar, checking the seal on a mobile command capsule. He looked exhausted, his eyes bloodshot under the fluorescent work lights.

"Priya says prepare the hardware for 2048," I said.

Wei spat onto the deck. "Priya isn't the one who has to calibrate thirty-two local ledger mirrors by tomorrow morning. Tell Fatima if she doesn't get me the cryptographic seeds by the end of the shift, I'm provisioning the capsule with blank drives."

Fatima was in the security annex, behind three layers of biometric clearance. I had to speak to her through the intercom grill.

"Wei needs the seeds," I said to the dark glass.

The dispenser slot clicked, ejecting a cold, heavy data slug. "Tell Aveline they’re authorized," Fatima’s voice crackled through the speaker. "But remind her that if these keys leak during transit, it’s her signature on the incident report, not mine."

Aveline was the floor supervisor, sitting at the high desk that overlooked the entire central administrative pool. She was the final gate before the logouts. I handed her the data slug.

"Fatima says it’s your signature on the line," I said.

Aveline took the slug, slotted it into the master console, and finally looked down at me. "It’s always my signature. Go home, recruit. Your shift ended four minutes ago. The system doesn't like unallocated overtime."

"Understood."

I walked back to my locker, the physical fatigue of a thousand small errands settling into my joints. I sat at the terminal, cleared the cache, and initialized the end-of-day sequence. The screen glowed white, reflecting off the dark glass of the empty office.

```
[SYSTEM LOGOUT SEQUENCE]
TIMESTAMP: 2026-06-14 // 21:05:23
LOCATION: SYNDICATE CENTRAL // CORE ADMINISTRATIVE POOL
ACTIVITY LOG: COMMITTED // 12/12 ROUTINE ROUTING COMPLETED
ERROR RATE: 0.00%

```

I placed my thumb on the scanner, authorizing the upload and sealing the daily record. I didn't type my name. The ledger didn't care about names. It just needed the ID.

```
[DE-8133 // AUTHORIZATION GRANTED]
[SESSION TERMINATED]

```

I grabbed my jacket and headed for the primary lift. Declan was already there, leaning against the rail, looking as worn out as the rest of the floor. The lift doors slid shut with a soft, pneumatic hiss, dropping us down toward the residential tiers.

Declan looked over, a faint smile breaking through his exhaustion.

"Later, Lieutenant Logic," he said.

"Catch you later designated driver".
